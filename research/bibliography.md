## Primary Textbooks

### Valli & Lucas (2000)
**Full Citation:**
Valli, C., & Lucas, C. (2000). *Linguistics of American Sign Language: An Introduction* (3rd ed.). Washington, D.C.: Gallaudet University Press.

**Relevance:** Foundational textbook for ASL linguistics. Covers phonology, morphology, syntax, and sociolinguistics.

**Key Chapters for Translation:**
- Chapter 7: Syntax (pp. 125-165) - Sentence structure, word order
- Chapter 8: Discourse (pp. 167-195) - Topic-comment, cohesion
- Chapter 5: Morphology (pp. 65-95) - How signs are modified

**ISBN:** 978-1563680977

---

### Baker-Shenk & Cokely (1980)
**Full Citation:**
Baker-Shenk, C., & Cokely, D. (1980). *American Sign Language: A Teacher's Resource Text on Grammar and Culture*. Silver Spring, MD: T.J. Publishers.

**Relevance:** Comprehensive grammar reference with detailed examples. Excellent for rule specification.

**Key Chapters:**
- Unit 6: Time (pp. 155-175) - Temporal expressions
- Unit 8: Questions (pp. 175-195) - Question formation
- Unit 10: Conditionals (pp. 210-230) - If-then structures

**Note:** Part of a multi-volume series; this is the Grammar and Culture volume.

---

### Sandler & Lillo-Martin (2006)
**Full Citation:**
Sandler, W., & Lillo-Martin, D. (2006). *Sign Language and Linguistic Universals*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.

**Relevance:** Cross-linguistic perspective on sign languages. Useful for understanding universal vs. language-specific features.

**Key Chapters:**
- Chapter 3: The Lexicon (pp. 75-120)
- Chapter 4: Morphology (pp. 121-180)
- Chapter 5: Syntax (pp. 181-260)

**ISBN:** 978-0521483957

---

## Research Papers & Articles

### Neidle et al. (2000)
**Full Citation:**
Neidle, C., Kegl, J., MacLaughlin, D., Bahan, B., & Lee, R. G. (2000). *The Syntax of American Sign Language: Functional Categories and Hierarchical Structure*. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press.

**Relevance:** Rigorous syntactic analysis of ASL. Essential for understanding formal structure.

**Key Sections:**
- Chapter 2: Basic Clause Structure (pp. 40-75)
- Chapter 3: WH-Questions (pp. 80-110)
- Chapter 4: Negation (pp. 115-145)

**ISBN:** 978-0262140676

---

### Liddell (2003)
**Full Citation:**
Liddell, S. K. (2003). *Grammar, Gesture, and Meaning in American Sign Language*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.

**Relevance:** Integrates gesture and grammar. Important for understanding non-manual markers.

**Key Chapters:**
- Chapter 2: Pointing (pp. 35-65)
- Chapter 5: Constructed Action (pp. 150-185)
- Chapter 8: Spatial Mapping (pp. 260-310)

**ISBN:** 978-0521016506

---

### Stokoe (1960)
**Full Citation:**
Stokoe, W. C. (1960). *Sign Language Structure: An Outline of the Visual Communication Systems of the American Deaf*. Studies in Linguistics, Occasional Papers 8. Buffalo: University of Buffalo.

**Relevance:** The foundational work that established ASL as a true language. Historical importance.

**Republished:** Stokoe, W. C. (2005). *Sign Language Structure* (2nd ed.). Silver Spring, MD: Linstok Press.

## Datasets

## Recommended Reading Order

For developers new to ASL linguistics:

1. **Start:** Lifeprint.com grammar pages (free, accessible)
2. **Intermediate:** Valli & Lucas (2000) Chapters 5, 7, 8
3. **Deep dive:** Neidle et al. (2000) for syntax details
4. **Advanced:** Liddell (2003) for spatial grammar

---

## Notes on Sources

- **Peer-reviewed > popular sources** for rule definitions
- **Native signer sources** preferred when available
- **Recent sources** for terminology (ASL linguistics vocabulary has evolved)
- **Cross-reference** important rules with multiple sources
